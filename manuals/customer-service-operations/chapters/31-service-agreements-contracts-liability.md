# Chapter 31 — Service Agreements, Contracts & Liability

A clearly written service agreement protects the shop legally, sets precise customer expectations, and reduces disputes before they begin. Every job above a defined threshold (recommended: $300) should be accompanied by a signed service authorization document.

## Elements of a Complete Service Authorization

<ul class="checklist">
<li>Customer full name, phone number, and email address</li>
<li>Vehicle year, make, model, color, VIN last 6 digits</li>
<li>Odometer reading at drop-off and pick-up</li>
<li>Itemized description of authorized services with individual pricing</li>
<li>Total authorized amount and deposit collected</li>
<li>Estimated completion date and communication method customer prefers</li>
<li>Pre-existing damage notation — signed by customer acknowledging the pre-existing conditions listed</li>
<li>Statement of work scope: "Additional work discovered during service will be communicated for approval before proceeding"</li>
<li>Liability limitations: "Shop is not responsible for pre-existing mechanical issues, paint defects concealed under dirt, or damage resulting from customer-directed work modifications"</li>
<li>Warranty terms summary (full warranty document provided separately)</li>
<li>Payment terms: when balance is due, accepted payment methods, late pickup fee policy</li>
<li>Customer signature and date</li>
</ul>

## Pre-Existing Damage Documentation Protocol

Photographing and documenting pre-existing damage is the single most important legal protection a shop has. A vehicle that arrives with a scratched bumper cannot be claimed by a customer to be the shop's fault if that damage is photographed, timestamped, and customer-acknowledged at intake.

<div class="procedure">

### Pre-Existing Damage Documentation

1. Perform the full walkaround under adequate lighting before moving the vehicle — natural light or 3-bulb LED preferred
2. Photograph all four corners, all four panels, the roof, hood, and all glass surfaces — minimum 20 photos
3. Use a raking-light inspection (low-angle flashlight) to highlight any paint defects, door dings, or swirl marks on the areas NOT being serviced
4. Note every defect on the damage diagram form — use standardized symbols: circle=chip, zigzag=scratch, triangle=dent
5. Take a photo of the completed damage diagram form next to the vehicle before the customer leaves
6. Email or text the damage documentation photos to the customer immediately — this creates a timestamped record
7. Have the customer sign the damage section of the service authorization form, confirming they acknowledge the pre-existing conditions
8. Store the documentation in the CRM record under the job, linked to the vehicle VIN

</div>

## Handling Liability Disputes

Even with thorough documentation, disputes will occasionally arise. Having a clear escalation and resolution process prevents small disagreements from becoming reviews or legal claims.

| Dispute Type | First Response | Evidence to Reference | Resolution Path |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Customer claims chip occurred at shop | Acknowledge concern; review photos together | Intake photos with timestamp | Show documented photo; if pre-existing, explain and offer good-will discount on repair referral |
| Customer claims swirl marks from wash | Inspect vehicle under proper lighting | Pre/post photos; confirm if correction service was purchased | If shop-caused, correct at no charge; if pre-existing, show documentation |
| Customer claims color is wrong | Compare to signed color authorization form | Signed sample approval or digital proof | Re-do if shop error; charge for change-of-mind revision |
| Customer disputes total price | Pull original signed authorization | Signed service authorization with itemized pricing | If authorization was signed, hold firm; offer payment plan if needed |
| Vehicle damage during service (shop fault) | Acknowledge immediately; do not minimize | Document damage; contact insurance if needed | Repair at shop expense; notify customer with plan before they discover it |

## Contract Language Reference

<div class="callout tip">

**Recommended warranty limitation language:**

"The warranty applies only to materials and workmanship under normal use and proper maintenance as described in the care instructions provided at delivery. Warranty is void if the vehicle has been involved in an accident, repainted, exposed to abrasive wash methods, or treated with products incompatible with the applied service."

**Recommended liability limitation language:**

"[Shop Name] exercises due care in handling all customer vehicles. However, [Shop Name] is not liable for pre-existing mechanical conditions, concealed paint defects, electronic malfunctions unrelated to service performed, or damage resulting from customer-requested modifications to the agreed scope of work."

</div>
