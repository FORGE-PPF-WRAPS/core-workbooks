# Chapter 5 — Consultation Framework

The consultation is the highest-leverage interaction in the sales process. Done well, it builds so much trust that price becomes secondary to the relationship. Done poorly, it leaves the customer feeling pressured, confused, or skeptical. This chapter provides a complete framework for conducting consultations that convert.

## Consultation Environment Setup

<ul class="checklist">
<li>The consultation area is clean and professional before the customer arrives</li>
<li>A physical or digital portfolio of past work is accessible</li>
<li>Sample materials (film swatches, coating demo panels, tint samples) are staged and ready</li>
<li>Water or coffee is offered as the customer arrives</li>
<li>The intake form is pre-populated with information already collected</li>
<li>The lead installer or CSM is available by phone for technical questions</li>
<li>No other staff conversations, music, or distractions compete for customer attention</li>
</ul>

## The Seven-Stage Consultation Framework

<div class="procedure">

### Consultation Framework

1. **Welcome & Rapport (2-3 min):** Greet by name. Thank them for coming in. Small talk on neutral topic. Offer refreshment.
2. **Vehicle Walk (5 min):** Walk the vehicle together. Let the customer point out what matters to them — don't lead yet.
3. **Needs Discovery (5-8 min):** Ask open-ended questions. Listen more than you speak. Uncover the "why."
4. **Education (5-10 min):** Teach them what they don't know. Use samples, demo panels, photos. Build credibility.
5. **Options Presentation (5-8 min):** Present two or three tiers — never just one option. Describe each as a solution to their stated goal.
6. **Investment Discussion (3-5 min):** Present pricing clearly and confidently. Tie it back to value, not cost.
7. **Next Steps (2-3 min):** Ask for the business or ask for permission to send an estimate. Give a clear timeline for follow-up.

</div>

## Needs Discovery — The 10 Essential Questions

These questions are not interrogation — they are the natural curiosity of someone who genuinely wants to find the right solution. Work them into conversation, not as a list.

| Question | What You're Learning |
| --- | --- |
| "What made you start thinking about this?" | Motivation, emotional driver |
| "How long have you had the vehicle?" | Attachment level, expected ownership duration |
| "Do you plan to keep it long-term or trade in a few years?" | Investment horizon — longer = higher-tier recommendation |
| "Where does it live when you're not driving it?" | Exposure level (garage vs. outdoor) |
| "Has it been through any repairs, repaints, or previous film?" | Existing condition; whether correction is needed |
| "What's your biggest concern — scratches, fading, rock chips, all of the above?" | Priority area for recommendation |
| "Have you had this type of service before?" | Experience level; calibrate explanation depth |
| "How important is maintaining the value for resale?" | ROI framing opportunity |
| "Is there a particular part of the car you're most worried about?" | May indicate a partial package is the right fit |
| "What does your ideal outcome look like?" | Sets the frame for the solution |

## Presenting Service Options

Always present options in order from most comprehensive to least — this anchors the customer to the top-tier first and makes mid-tier feel like a reasonable compromise rather than the expensive choice.

<div class="callout tip">

**The Three-Option Framework:**

**Option A (Premium):** Full vehicle coverage, top-tier film/coating, extended warranty — "This is the gold standard."

**Option B (Core):** High-impact zones, mid-tier product, standard warranty — "This is what most of our clients choose for this type of vehicle."

**Option C (Entry):** Targeted protection on the highest-impact areas only — "If budget is the primary factor, this is where I'd start."

</div>

## Handling Hesitation at Consultation

Some customers need time or information before deciding. Never pressure; always support.

<div class="procedure">

### When a Customer Asks for Time

1. Acknowledge the decision genuinely: "Absolutely — this is a real investment in your vehicle."
2. Identify the information gap: "Is there anything you'd like me to clarify or go over again?"
3. Offer a specific follow-up: "I'll get your detailed written estimate over to you by end of business today. Would tomorrow afternoon work to connect briefly?"
4. Leave them with something tangible: samples, a printed overview, a QR code to your portfolio
5. Log the outcome in CRM and set the follow-up task before they leave the parking lot

</div>
