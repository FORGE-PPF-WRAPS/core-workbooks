# Chapter 12 — Seam Management

Seam management is a design and execution discipline. All wrapped vehicles have seams — the installer's skill lies in placing them where they are least visible and executing them so cleanly that even a visible seam does not look like an error.

## Seam Placement Hierarchy

<div class="procedure">

### Prioritize Seam Locations in This Order

1. **First choice:** Factory body gap (door-to-fender, trunk-to-quarter, hood-to-fender) — seam hides completely in the gap
2. **Second choice:** Character line (horizontal or vertical feature pressed into the body) — the line itself draws the eye; seam blends into the shadow
3. **Third choice:** Under trim or molding — film terminates behind a trim strip; never visible
4. **Fourth choice:** Edge of a panel where two different film colors or finishes are designed to meet — a design seam is an intentional joint
5. **Last resort:** Open panel face — only when film width is insufficient and no design opportunity exists; minimize visibility with perfect grain matching

</div>

## Executing a Butt-Join Seam

<div class="procedure">

### Butt-Join Seam on Panel Face

1. Apply the first film piece; squeegee fully; do not post-heat yet; leave a 5 mm uncut edge at the seam line
2. Apply the second film piece, overlapping the first by 5–8 mm at the seam line
3. Squeegee the second piece fully
4. Apply knifeless tape under the overlap (between the two layers) along the desired seam line before applying the second piece, OR cut through both layers simultaneously with a channel cutter at the overlap
5. Pull the knifeless tape or remove the cut strips from both layers
6. Post-heat the seam area; squeegee firmly so both edges lie flat
7. Inspect under a raking light: no gap, no ridge, no adhesive visible at the joint
8. Apply edge sealer along the seam if it is in a high-stress area (curves, high-traffic zone)

</div>

## Overlap vs. Butt-Join — When to Use Each

| Joint Type | Description | Use When | Avoid When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Butt-join | Two film edges meet precisely; no overlap | High-visibility seam locations; color-change wraps; anywhere appearance is paramount | High-stress mechanical areas; edges that will see water infiltration |
| Overlap | One film edge laps over another by 3–5 mm | Concealed areas (under trim, inside jambs); fleet wraps where production speed matters | Visible panel faces; matte and textured films (step is very visible) |
| Knifeless seam through both layers | Both films applied with overlap; tape cuts both simultaneously | Any visible face seam; ensures perfect alignment of the joint | Cannot be used near glass or painted surfaces where the mono-filament could scratch |
