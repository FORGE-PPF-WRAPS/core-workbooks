# Chapter 35 — Collector & Stored Vehicle Detailing

A growing segment of the detailing market involves vehicles that are stored rather than driven — classic cars, collector vehicles, exotic supercars kept in climate-controlled facilities, and seasonal-use vehicles. Detailing for storage requires a fundamentally different approach than detailing for daily use: the goal is to protect the vehicle from dormancy-related deterioration and ensure it is in pristine condition when the owner returns.

## Pre-Storage Detail Checklist

<ul class="checklist">
<li>Exterior wash and decontamination: remove all road contamination, bird drops, and industrial fallout before storage — these continue to etch paint under a cover</li>
<li>Paint protection application or refresh: if the vehicle has no ceramic coating, apply a sealant or wax before storage; if coated, perform decon wash and top-up</li>
<li>Glass: clean all exterior glass surfaces; apply glass coating if not already coated — glass water spots are almost impossible to remove without polishing after long storage</li>
<li>Rubber seals and trim: condition all exterior rubber with a UV-protective rubber conditioner — dry rubber cracks over storage</li>
<li>Wheels and tires: clean thoroughly including inner barrel; apply tire protectant; inflate tires to the upper end of the manufacturer range to compensate for pressure loss during storage</li>
<li>Interior: vacuum all surfaces; extract any moisture from carpets; treat leather with a conditioner; place a desiccant pack inside the vehicle to absorb moisture</li>
<li>Engine bay: light cleaning; check for any current fluid leaks that should be resolved before storage</li>
<li>Final inspection and photo documentation: photograph the vehicle from all angles immediately before storage — this is the baseline for any insurance or condition dispute</li>
</ul>

## Post-Storage Inspection and Detailing

<div class="procedure">

### Post-Storage Detail Sequence

1. Remove any cover carefully — static charge on the cover can attract dust and cause scratching if the cover is dragged across the paint
2. Inspect the cover itself for signs of moisture trapping, mold, or debris — a contaminated cover should not be replaced on the vehicle
3. Visual inspection of the vehicle before any washing: look for signs of pest activity, moisture intrusion, fluid leaks, or tire flat-spotting
4. Exterior wash using the two-bucket method or rinse-less wash if the vehicle was stored under a cover in a clean environment
5. Inspect paint surface under inspection lighting: storage can cause moisture spotting, cover transfer marks, or adhesion failures in wax or sealant
6. Correct any surface issues found before applying fresh protection
7. Interior inspection: check for moisture damage to leather, mold in carpets or under floor mats, or pest evidence
8. Road-test preparation check: verify tire pressure, check fluid levels, confirm the vehicle is safe to operate before delivery to the customer

</div>

## Long-Term Storage Paint Maintenance Programs

Collector car facilities and high-end storage operations represent a niche but high-value recurring service opportunity. A vehicle in storage for 6+ months benefits from scheduled mid-storage visits from the detailer, even without the owner present.

| Service Program | Frequency | What Is Performed | Pricing Model |

| Monthly storage inspection | Monthly | Visual inspection; cover check; report to owner with photos | Retainer: $50–$100/month |
