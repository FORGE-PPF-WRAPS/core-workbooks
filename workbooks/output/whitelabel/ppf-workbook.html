Welcome

Welcome to the Skill Forge Paint Protection Film (PPF) Core Course. Over three intensive days you will learn the foundational skills to install automotive paint protection film — from surface preparation and your first flat-panel install to completing a professional partial-front package under supervision.

PPF is one of the highest-value services in automotive restyling. This course gives you the hands-on practice and quality standards to start offering PPF with confidence.

What "Core" means: This course is designed for beginners with little or no PPF experience. Vinyl wrap or window tint experience helps but is not required. By Day 3 you will have completed multiple panel installs and a supervised partial-front package. Advanced techniques (full vehicle wraps, color PPF, complex bumpers) are covered in our Advanced courses.

Session 1: PPF Film Types & Construction

What Is Paint Protection Film?

PPF is a thick, optically clear (or colored/textured) polyurethane film applied to painted surfaces to protect against rock chips, scratches, bug acids, bird droppings, and UV damage. Modern films are TPU (thermoplastic polyurethane) — flexible, self-healing, and durable.

Film Construction

Layer Function
Top coat Self-healing, stain resistance, hydrophobic properties
TPU body Impact absorption, flexibility, clarity
Adhesive Pressure-sensitive; designed for clean removal without paint damage
Release liner Protects adhesive until install

Film Categories

Type Description Common Use
Standard gloss Crystal clear, high gloss Full front, high-impact zones
Matte / satin Reduces gloss of factory paint Matte wraps, stealth packages
Colored PPF Pigmented TPU Color-change protection
Textured Carbon fiber look, etc. Accent panels, trim

Installer tip: Self-healing works with heat — light scratches disappear with sun exposure or warm water. Explain this to customers; it's a major selling point for premium films.

Self-Healing Explained

The top coat is an elastomeric polymer that flows back into place when heated above ~140°F. This is not magic — deep gouges that penetrate the TPU body cannot self-heal. Set customer expectations accurately.

Session 2: Paint Assessment & Correction Basics

Before Any PPF Install, Inspect Paint

Walk the vehicle in good lighting (preferably outdoors or with LED inspection lights):

  • Rock chips and deep scratches — document pre-existing damage
  • Swirl marks and oxidation — may need polish before film
  • Repainted panels — adhesion can vary; test or disclose risk
  • Contamination — tar, tree sap, rail dust, iron particles
  • PPF-unfriendly surfaces — fresh paint (cure time), vinyl wraps, plasti-dip

Paint Correction Decision

Condition Action
Light swirls, film will hide minor defects Wash, decon, alcohol wipe — proceed
Moderate swirls visible through film Single-stage polish recommended
Deep scratches, chips to primer Document; fill chips if needed; disclose to customer
Fresh paint (< 30 days) Do not install — outgas will cause adhesive failure

Always photograph pre-existing damage. PPF makes paint look better — customers may blame you for damage that was already there.

Decontamination Sequence (Standard)

  1. Wash — two-bucket method, clean mitts, blow out emblems and gaps
  2. Clay bar or clay mitt — remove bonded contamination with lubricant
  3. Iron remover — dissolve embedded ferrous particles (purple reaction = working)
  4. Tar/sap remover — spot treat as needed
  5. Isopropyl alcohol wipe (70% IPA) — remove oils and residue; final prep
  6. Inspect under light — any remaining particles must be removed before film

Session 3: Tools, Slip Solution & Workspace

Essential PPF Tools

Tool Purpose
Hard squeegee (with felt or low-friction sleeve) Primary wet-out tool
Soft squeegee Final passes, delicate areas
Slip/tack solution bottles Positioning and final adhesion
Heat gun Stretching, conforming, post-heating
Snitty knife / plotter blade Trimming film edges
Extension blade holder Precise edge trimming in tight areas
Tack cloth / lint roller Remove dust before film contact
Plotter (shop) Pre-cut patterns — introduced Day 3
Light bar / inspection lamp Reveal contamination and defects

Slip vs. Tack Solution

Solution Purpose When
Slip (soapy water) Allows film to slide for positioning During placement and initial squeegee
Tack (higher alcohol or dedicated tack solution) Increases adhesion for final tack Edges, wraps, and problem areas

Follow your film manufacturer's recommended ratios. Too much slip = film won't stick. Too much tack too early = can't reposition.

Workspace Requirements

  • Temperature: 65–75°F ideal for adhesive performance
  • Dust control: avoid active sanding nearby; wet-mop floors
  • Lighting: bright, angled light reveals every particle
  • Organized film: rolls stored flat or on rack; handle edges carefully

Session 4: Safety

Non-negotiable safety rules:

  1. Blades cut paint and skin — use fresh blades; cut away from body
  2. Heat guns reach extreme temperatures — never hold stationary on paint or film
  3. Chemical PPE: gloves and eye protection when using IPA, iron remover, and adhesives
  4. Never install on a hot panel in direct sun — adhesive behaves unpredictably
  5. Lift panels properly — hoods and doors are heavy; use props and assistance

Lab 1: Flat Panel Install — Hood Section

Lab 1 — Hood Section Install

Objective: Prep a hood section and install a piece of PPF with clean wet-out and no trapped contamination.

Time: 90 minutes

Part A — Decon & Prep (25 min)

  1. Wash and clay the hood section
  2. Iron remover if needed; rinse and dry
  3. IPA wipe the entire install zone
  4. Final inspection under light — zero particles allowed
  5. Checkpoint: Panel squeaks clean under your hand

Part B — Film Handling & Position (20 min)

  1. Cut film from roll with 1–2" margin beyond panel edges
  2. Peel liner partially; spray adhesive side with slip solution
  3. Spray panel with slip solution
  4. Position film; align with body lines or panel edges
  5. Checkpoint: Film slides freely for final positioning

Part C — Wet-Out (35 min)

  1. Anchor the center — light tack squeegee pass in the middle
  2. Work outward in overlapping strokes; push solution to edges
  3. Use firm, even pressure — PPF is thicker than tint
  4. Post-heat lightly if minor fingers remain (instructor guidance)
  5. Checkpoint: No bubbles, no visible contamination, film fully adhered

Lab 2: Edge Work — Wrap, Tuck & Trim

Lab 2 — Edge Techniques

Objective: Practice the three primary edge finishing methods on a practice panel.

Time: 75 minutes

Edge Method 1 — Cut to Edge (Flush)

  • Film trimmed exactly at panel edge
  • Clean look; no wrap
  • Requires precise cutting; edge is vulnerable to lift over time

Edge Method 2 — Wrap Edge

  • Film wraps 2–3mm around panel edge to backside
  • More durable; preferred on hoods, fenders, doors
  • Use heat to conform; squeegee wrap zone firmly

Edge Method 3 — Tuck into Gap

  • Film tucked into panel gaps (hood-to-fender, door jambs)
  • Invisible edge; premium finish
  • Requires gap access and precise trimming with extension blade

Practice all three methods on your assigned panel sections.

Session 5: Stretching, Anchors & Relief Cuts

Controlled Stretch

PPF can stretch 10–40% depending on film brand and technique. Controlled stretch means applying tension only where needed to conform to curves — not stretching the entire panel uniformly.

Anchor Point Strategy

  1. Identify the flattest zone — anchor here first (center of hood, flat bumper section)
  2. Work toward curves — stretch incrementally as you squeegee outward
  3. Heat assists stretch — warm film conforms without excessive force
  4. Never stretch beyond manufacturer limit — film thins, adhesives stress, silvering appears

Relief Cuts

A relief cut is a carefully placed slit in excess film (not in the installed area) that allows the film to open and conform around sharp compound curves, mirror bases, and badge areas.

Rules for relief cuts:

  • Cut in excess margin only — never in the visible install zone
  • Cut from the edge inward — stops before the adhered area
  • One cut at a time — reassess after each
  • Seal cut edges with heat and tack solution after conforming

Silvering (whitish haze along adhesive) means the film was overstretched or adhesive was stressed. It may not disappear. Remove and replace the section.

Session 6: Bumpers, Mirrors & Complex Curves

Bumper Strategy

Bumpers are the most complex PPF install zone — compound curves, sensor cutouts, and tight edges.

  1. Pattern vs. bulk — beginners should use plotter patterns when available
  2. Work from center outward — same anchor principle as hood
  3. Heat frequently — short bursts, keep moving
  4. Sensor and camera cutouts — punch or trim precisely; don't cover ADAS sensors unless customer waives (not recommended)
  5. Relief cuts are normal on aggressive bumper curves — hide them in inconspicuous zones

Mirror Caps

  1. Remove cap if possible — flat install on bench is easier
  2. If on-vehicle: stretch from center; heavy heat on compound curves
  3. Wrap edges for durability — mirror caps see abrasion
  4. Post-heat entire cap after install

Session 7: Troubleshooting Common Defects

Defect Likely Cause Fix
Finger Insufficient stretch/heat on curve Lift, reheat, re-squeegee; or remove and redo
Silvering Overstretch or adhesive stress Remove section; install fresh film
Contamination Skipped decon step or dusty shop Remove film, re-prep, reinstall
Edge lift Incomplete tack on wrap zone Tack solution + firm squeegee + post-heat
Stretch marks Film pulled too hard Remove and replace — marks don't heal
Orange peel in adhesive Trapped solution Squeegee harder; post-heat; severe cases = redo

Session 9: Pattern Software & Workflow

Bulk Roll vs. Plotter Pattern

Method Pros Cons
Bulk roll No software cost; full control More waste; slower; harder on complex panels
Plotter pattern Precise fit; faster; less waste Software subscription; database gaps for new models

Beginner Workflow (Plotter)

  1. Select vehicle year/make/model/trim in pattern software
  2. Choose kit (partial front, full hood, etc.)
  3. Nest patterns on roll for material efficiency
  4. Plot/cut on machine
  5. Weed liner from cut pieces
  6. Install each piece in logical order: center panels first, then edges, then details

Pattern Software at Your Shop

Your shop will use one plotter pattern platform. Learn that system deeply — vehicle lookup, kit selection, nesting, and cut workflow. Ask your instructor which platform you will train on and where to find vehicle patterns for new models.

Session 10: QC Inspection & Customer Delivery

QC Checklist — Every Install

  • Paint prepped — decon and IPA complete; pre-existing damage documented
  • No contamination under film (inspect with light at angle)
  • No fingers, silvering, or stretch marks in visible areas
  • Edges wrapped, tucked, or flushed per spec — no lift
  • All plotter pieces aligned — no visible seams misaligned at panel lines
  • Emblems, badges, and sensors properly handled
  • ADAS sensors uncovered and functional
  • Post-heat completed on all installed panels
  • Customer paperwork with package details and warranty info

Customer Delivery

  1. Walk the vehicle with the customer in good lighting
  2. Point out coverage zones and any documented pre-existing paint damage
  3. Explain cure time: no wash for 48 hours; hand wash only for first week
  4. Provide written aftercare: no pressure washer directly on edges, no abrasive compounds on film
  5. Explain warranty: film warranty (manufacturer) vs. workmanship warranty (your shop)
