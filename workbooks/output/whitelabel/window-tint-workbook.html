Welcome

Welcome to the Skill Forge Window Tint Core Course. Over three intensive days you will build the foundational knowledge and hands-on skills needed to install automotive window film professionally — from your first flat-glass cut to a complete vehicle tint job.

This workbook is your companion through classroom sessions, shop demonstrations, and supervised installs. Complete the knowledge checks, take notes during labs, and use the checklists to track your progress toward course completion.

What "Core" means: This course is designed for beginners with little or no tint experience. By Day 3 you will have installed film on multiple glass types and completed a supervised full-vehicle tint. Advanced techniques (ceramic specialty installs, commercial fleet work, windshield full-tint where permitted) are covered in our Advanced courses.

Session 1: Window Film Types & Performance

How Window Film Works

Automotive window film is a multi-layer laminate applied to the interior surface of glass. Key layers include:

  • Scratch-resistant hard coat — protects the film surface from abrasion
  • Tinted or coated layer — provides UV rejection, heat rejection, and visible light reduction
  • Adhesive layer — bonds film to glass (pressure-sensitive or water-activated depending on product)
  • Release liner — protects adhesive until installation

Common Film Categories

Type Construction Heat Rejection Notes
Dyed Dyed layer in polyester Moderate Economical; can fade over time in harsh sun
Metalized Metal particles in film Good Can interfere with radio/GPS/cell signals
Hybrid Dyed + metalized Good Balance of performance and cost
Carbon Carbon particles Very good No signal interference; good color stability
Ceramic Ceramic nano-particles Excellent Premium tier; best heat rejection, no signal issues

Installer tip: Learn to explain film tiers in customer language: "This film blocks 99% of UV rays" is more meaningful to most customers than quoting IR rejection percentages.

Key Performance Metrics

  • VLT (Visible Light Transmission) — percentage of visible light passing through the film + glass assembly. Lower VLT = darker appearance.
  • UV rejection — quality automotive films block 99%+ of UV rays regardless of VLT.
  • TSER (Total Solar Energy Rejected) — overall heat rejection; important for upselling ceramic.
  • IRR (Infrared Rejection) — measures rejection of infrared heat; ceramic films excel here.

Session 2: Tint Laws & Customer Consultation

Understanding VLT Limits

Every state (and many municipalities) regulates how dark window tint can be on each window position. Limits are expressed as minimum VLT — the percentage of light that must pass through.

Typical window positions regulated:

  • Front driver & passenger (side)
  • Front windshield (often only brow/visor strip allowed)
  • Rear side windows
  • Rear window (back glass)

Critical: Film VLT ratings are measured on the film alone. Installed VLT on glass is affected by the glass itself. Always consult current state law and your film manufacturer's installed VLT data before advising customers.

Customer Consultation Basics

Before any install, confirm:

  1. Legal limits for their state and which windows they want tinted
  2. Film tier — dyed, carbon, ceramic — and explain the performance difference
  3. Warranty — film warranty vs. your shop's workmanship warranty
  4. Cure time — windows stay up 24–72 hours depending on film and weather
  5. Existing tint — old film must be removed before new install (additional charge)

Document everything. A signed work order with film type, VLT, windows to be tinted, and price prevents disputes.

Slip Solution Recipe (General)

Most shops use a mild soap-and-water mix:

  • 1 quart distilled water
  • 2–4 drops mild soap or your film supplier's recommended slip agent

Too much soap = film slides excessively and won't grab. Too little = film sticks before positioned.

Workspace Requirements

  • Clean floor — lint and dust are the enemy
  • Good lighting — LED shop lights at the glass surface reveal contamination instantly
  • Climate control — ideal install temp 50–90°F; avoid dusty or windy conditions with doors open
  • Organized film storage — rolls upright, climate-controlled, away from dust

Session 4: Safety

Non-negotiable safety rules for every install:

  1. Heat guns reach 1,000°F+ — never point at skin, wiring, or interior trim
  2. Razor blades cut glass AND skin — always cut away from your body
  3. Ammonia-based cleaners can damage film adhesive and interior surfaces
  4. Defroster lines are fragile — excessive blade pressure or heat can break them
  5. Wear eye protection when using heat guns near shrinking film

Chemical Safety

  • Know your SDS (Safety Data Sheet) for all cleaning products
  • Ventilate the workspace when using strong degreasers
  • Never mix chemicals unless the manufacturer specifies it

Lab 1: Glass Prep & Flat Glass Install

Lab 1 — Flat Glass Prep & Install

Objective: Prepare a flat practice glass panel and install a piece of window film with clean edges and minimal contamination.

Time: 90 minutes

Part A — Glass Prep (20 min)

  1. Inspect glass under shop lighting for existing contamination, adhesive residue, or defects
  2. Spray with slip solution and scrub with tint-safe pad
  3. Squeegee dry completely — water trapped under film causes bubbles
  4. Final pass with lint-free cloth; inspect for remaining particles
  5. Checkpoint: Glass should be squeaky-clean. Run your fingernail along the surface — if you feel grit, re-prep.

Part B — Cut & Position (25 min)

  1. Unroll film on the outside of the glass (liner facing you)
  2. Cut film roughly 1" larger than glass on all sides
  3. Peel liner partially; spray adhesive side with slip solution
  4. Spray glass interior with slip solution
  5. Position film on interior glass; align edges with slight overhang
  6. Checkpoint: Film should slide freely for positioning

Part C — Wet-Out & Squeegee (35 min)

  1. Starting from the center, squeegee outward in firm, overlapping strokes
  2. Push solution and air to the edges — do not trap air in the center
  3. Tack down edges; trim excess with fresh blade using glass as guide
  4. Final light pass with soft squeegee
  5. Checkpoint: No visible bubbles larger than a pinhead; edges fully adhered

Part D — Self-Assessment

Rate your install (1–5): Edge cleanliness ___ | Bubble count ___ | Contamination ___

Lab 2: Pattern Cutting from Roll

Lab 2 — Roll Pattern Cutting

Objective: Cut a pattern for a door glass using the outside-cut method.

Time: 60 minutes

Outside Cut Method

  1. Place film on exterior glass with liner out
  2. Trim excess to manageable size
  3. Cut to glass shape using the glass edge as your guide
  4. Peel liner, wet adhesive, transfer to interior
  5. Wet-out and finish

Inside Cut Method (preview)

  1. Place film inside glass, liner out
  2. Cut to fit from interior — more precise but tighter workspace
  3. Used commonly on rear windows and final trims

Practice both methods on your door glass assignment during Day 2.

Session 5: Heat Shrinking Theory

Why Shrink?

Flat film does not naturally conform to compound curves (bowed rear windows, curved door glass). Shrinking uses heat to relax the polyester film so it contracts and conforms to the curve without fingers (lifted areas that won't adhere).

The Shrink Process

  1. Place film on the outside of the curved glass (liner out)
  2. Identify low points and fingers that need shrinkage
  3. Apply heat in sweeping motions — never hold stationary
  4. Film will wrinkle, then tighten — stop before overheating
  5. Smooth with gloved hand or squeegee; repeat until film lays flat
  6. Cut pattern, then transfer to interior for install

Overheating causes: film discoloration, adhesive failure, and visible distortion. If you see rainbow haziness, you've gone too far. Start over with fresh film.

Heat Gun Technique

  • Keep nozzle 4–6 inches from film surface
  • Use continuous sweeping motion
  • Shrink from the center of fingers toward edges
  • Let film cool before cutting — shrinking continues slightly as it cools

Session 6: Door Glass & Defroster Lines

Door Glass Access Methods

Method When to Use Notes
Drop glass Most modern vehicles Lower glass 2–3" for bottom edge access
Panel removal Tight seals, some trucks/SUVs Remove door panel; protect clips and wiring
Full glass removal Deep tint, problem installs Requires urethane/seal knowledge — advanced

Working Around Defroster Lines

Rear windows and some side glass have defroster lines (conductive ceramic frit lines):

  • Never scrape across lines with a blade
  • Squeegee parallel to lines, not across them
  • Use a soft squeegee with light pressure over lines
  • Heat shrinking over lines requires extra care — lines are raised

Session 7: Troubleshooting Common Defects

Defect Likely Cause Fix
Finger Insufficient shrink on curve Remove film; re-shrink or start fresh
Bubble Trapped air/solution Small: pin and squeegee; large: remove and redo
Contamination Dirty glass or dusty environment Remove film, re-prep glass, reinstall
Crease Film folded during handling Cannot fix — replace film
Dust speck Airborne particle during install Pin if tiny; redo if visible
Edge lift Incomplete squeegee at edge Re-squeegee; heat lightly if needed

The 24-hour rule: Minor haziness from moisture in the slip solution often clears within 24–48 hours as the adhesive cures. Don't panic-rework a good install too soon.

Lab 3 — Curved Glass Install

Objective: Shrink and install film on a curved practice panel or quarter window.

Time: 90 minutes

  1. Place film on exterior curved glass; identify fingers

  3. Allow to cool; cut pattern with 1/16" margin
  4. Transfer to interior; wet-out from center outward
  5. Pay attention to edges — light heat may be needed for final tack
  6. Checkpoint: No fingers remaining; edges fully adhered; no creases

Session 8: Rear Window Strategy

The rear window is the most challenging automotive glass install because of its compound curve. Strategy:

  1. Choose the right film — some films shrink easier than others; ask your film rep
  2. One-piece vs. seamed — one-piece looks best; seams are a last resort on extreme curves
  3. Shrink in stages — tackle the deepest curve areas first
  4. Use plenty of slip on interior install — rear windows have tight access
  5. H-pattern shrink — common technique for deep bowl rear windows (instructor demo)

Windshield Brow (Visor Strip)

Where legal, a brow strip on the windshield top is a common add-on:

  • Typical depth: 4–6 inches below the shade band
  • Cut from flat film; no shrink usually required
  • Align with rearview mirror sight line — confirm with customer seated in vehicle

Session 9: Quality Control Inspection

QC Checklist — Every Install

  • Glass prepped — no visible contamination under film
  • Film positioned correctly — no gaps at edges
  • Edges cleanly trimmed — no ragged cuts
  • No fingers on curved areas
  • No bubbles larger than pinhead (customer-visible)
  • Defroster lines intact — none broken or scraped
  • Interior trim and panels reinstalled without damage
  • Customer paperwork completed with film type and VLT documented

Rework Decision Tree

  • Cosmetic defect visible from 3 feet → rework before delivery
  • Edge dust speck, customer won't see → document and deliver with care instructions
  • Functional issue (edge lift, finger in sight line) → always rework

Session 10: Pricing & Business Basics

Common Pricing Models

Model Description
Per window Price each position separately
Package 2-front, 4-side, 5-window, 7-window packages
Film tier upsell Base price for dyed; +$ for carbon; +$$ for ceramic

What to Include in Your Price

  • Film cost (waste factor ~10–15%)
  • Labor time (your hourly rate × estimated hours)
  • Overhead (rent, utilities, insurance, tools)
  • Profit margin (target 40–60% gross on labor)

Aftercare Instructions (Give Every Customer)

  1. Keep windows up for 48–72 hours (follow film manufacturer spec)
  2. Clean with ammonia-free cleaner and soft cloth only
  3. Avoid abrasive pads on tinted windows
  4. Small water bubbles are normal during cure — they will dissipate
  5. Contact shop if edge lift occurs within workmanship warranty period
