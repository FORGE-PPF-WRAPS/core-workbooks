## Session 1: Vinyl Film Types & Construction

### What Is Automotive Wrap Film?

Automotive wrap film is a conformable vinyl laminate applied to painted surfaces for color change, accent styling, or brand graphics. Unlike PPF, wrap film is designed primarily for aesthetics — though quality cast films also provide UV and minor abrasion protection.

### Film Construction

| Layer | Function |
|-------|----------|
| **Face film / top coat** | Color, gloss, or texture; UV and scratch resistance |
| **Vinyl body** | Conformability, opacity, dimensional stability |
| **Adhesive** | Pressure-sensitive; designed for clean removal within rated lifespan |
| **Release liner** | Protects adhesive until install |

### Cast vs. Calendered Vinyl

| Type | Manufacturing | Characteristics | Best Use |
|------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| **Cast vinyl** | Liquid resin poured on casting sheet; cured | Thin, stable, conforms to complex curves, minimal shrink | Full vehicle wraps, hoods, bumpers, long-term installs |
| **Calendered vinyl** | Heated PVC rolled through calender rollers | Thicker, less conformable, shrinks over time | Flat signage, fleet graphics on box trucks, short-term promos |

<div class="callout tip">

**Installer tip:** Always use **cast vinyl** for automotive body panels. Calendered film on compound curves will shrink, lift, and fail within months. If a customer brings their own material, verify it is automotive-grade cast before accepting the job.

</div>

### Common Finishes

Gloss, satin/matte, metallic/pearl, color-shift specialty, and textured (carbon fiber look) films are all available in cast construction. Metallic and pearl finishes show stretch and require careful post-heating; light colors reveal paint imperfections more than dark colors.

## Session 2: Paint Assessment & Correction Basics

### Before Any Wrap Install, Inspect Paint

Walk the vehicle in good lighting (preferably outdoors or with LED inspection lights):

<ul class="checklist">
<li>Rock chips and deep scratches — document pre-existing damage; may show through light colors</li>
<li>Swirl marks and oxidation — will telegraph through gloss and metallic films</li>
<li>Repainted panels — adhesion can vary; test or disclose risk</li>
<li>Contamination — tar, tree sap, rail dust, iron particles, wax residue</li>
<li>Wrap-unfriendly surfaces — fresh paint (cure time), failing clear coat, rust, plasti-dip</li>
<li>Existing PPF or partial wraps — removal may be required; factor time and cost</li>
</ul>

### Paint Correction Decision

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Light swirls, solid color film will hide minor defects | Wash, decon, alcohol wipe — proceed |
| Moderate swirls visible through film (especially gloss/metallic) | Single-stage polish recommended |
| Deep scratches, chips to primer | Document; fill chips if needed; disclose to customer |
| Fresh paint (< 30 days) | Do not install — outgas will cause adhesive failure and bubbles |
| Failing clear coat | Do not wrap — film will lift with failing paint |

<div class="callout warning">

**Always photograph pre-existing damage.** Wrap makes paint look better — customers may blame you for damage that was already there. Light-colored films reveal imperfections that dark colors hide.

</div>

### Decontamination Sequence (Standard)

1. **Wash** — two-bucket method, clean mitts, blow out emblems and gaps
2. **Clay bar or clay mitt** — remove bonded contamination with lubricant
3. **Iron remover** — dissolve embedded ferrous particles (purple reaction = working)
4. **Tar/sap remover** — spot treat as needed
5. **Isopropyl alcohol wipe (70% IPA)** — remove oils, wax, and residue; final prep
6. **Inspect under light** — any remaining particles must be removed before film contact

## Session 3: Tools, Application Fluid & Workspace

### Essential Wrap Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Felt-edge squeegee** | Primary wet-out tool; prevents scratching face film |
| **Hard card / plastic squeegee** | Tucking into gaps; firm pressure on edges |
| **Soft squeegee / wrap glove** | Final passes, delicate areas, large flat panels |
| **Heat gun** | Stretching, conforming, post-heating, memory reset |
| **Snitty knife / plotter blade** | Trimming film edges |
| **Extension blade holder** | Precise edge trimming in tight areas |
| **Knifeless tape** | Cut film at panel lines without blade on paint |
| **Tape measure** | Panel sizing, material estimation, alignment |
| **Application fluid bottle** | Positioning solution for wet apply or repositioning |
| **Tack cloth / lint roller** | Remove dust before film contact |
| **Light bar / inspection lamp** | Reveal contamination, stretch marks, and defects |

### Knifeless Tape

Knifeless tape creates a safe cut line on the vehicle without running a blade directly on paint. A filament runs along the tape edge — pull the filament after film is applied and it cuts through the vinyl cleanly.

![Knifeless tape spool — filament cut system for safe panel-line trimming](workbooks/assets/images/vinyl-wrap/tools/knifeless-tape-spool.jpg)

Use at hood-to-fender lines, door gaps, and anywhere a blade slip would damage paint.

### Application Fluid vs. Dry Apply

| Method | Purpose | When |
|--------|---------|------|
| **Application fluid** (slip solution) | Allows film to slide for positioning | Complex panels, beginner installs, repositioning |
| **Dry apply** | Immediate tack; faster on flat panels | Experienced installers, flat zones, some manufacturer specs |

**Follow your film manufacturer's recommended method.** Too much fluid = adhesive failure and edge lift. Not enough = cannot reposition on complex panels.

### Workspace Requirements

- Temperature: 65–75°F ideal for adhesive performance and conformability
- Dust control: avoid active sanding nearby; wet-mop floors; keep doors closed when possible
- Lighting: bright, angled light reveals every particle and stretch mark
- Organized film: rolls stored upright or on rack; handle edges carefully; note lot numbers for warranty

## Session 4: Safety

<div class="callout warning">

**Non-negotiable safety rules:**

1. Blades cut paint and skin — use fresh blades; cut away from body and hands
2. Heat guns reach 1,000°F+ — never hold stationary on paint, plastic, or film; keep moving
3. Chemical PPE: gloves and eye protection when using IPA, iron remover, and adhesive removers
4. Never install on a hot panel in direct sun — adhesive behaves unpredictably; film stretches unevenly
5. Lift panels properly — hoods and doors are heavy; use props and assistance
6. Ventilation — post-heating produces fumes; ensure adequate airflow in the bay

</div>

## Session 5: Stretching, Anchors & Post-Heating

### Controlled Stretch

Cast vinyl can stretch 10–30% depending on film type and technique. **Controlled stretch** means applying tension only where needed to conform to curves — not stretching the entire panel uniformly.

### Anchor Point Strategy

Anchor the flattest zone first (center of hood or door), work toward curves with incremental stretch, use heat to assist, and never exceed manufacturer stretch limits — metallic films will color-shift and silver if overstretched.

### Post-Heating — Critical Step

Post-heating resets the film's memory so it holds the new shape without shrinking back.

**Post-heat process:** Complete wet-out → heat film evenly at 6–8" with constant motion → target ~180–200°F on edges, overlaps, and stretched zones → do not overheat.

<div class="callout warning">

**Silvering** (whitish haze along adhesive) means the film was overstretched or adhesive was stressed. It may not disappear. Remove and replace the section.

</div>

### Relief Cuts (When Needed)

Relief cuts in **excess margin only** allow film to open around sharp compound curves. Cut from the edge inward one at a time; seal with heat and firm squeegee after conforming. Never cut in the visible install zone.

---

## Session 6: Bumpers, Mirrors & Complex Curves

### Bumper Strategy

Bumpers are the most complex wrap zone — compound curves, sensor cutouts, and tight edges.

1. **Pattern vs. bulk** — beginners should use plotter patterns when available
2. **Work from center outward** — same anchor principle as hood
3. **Heat frequently** — short bursts, keep moving
4. **Post-heat entire bumper** — edges and deep curves are shrink-prone

### Mirror Caps

Remove cap when possible for bench install. On-vehicle: stretch from center, heavy heat on curves, wrap edges, post-heat entire cap.

### Hood, Door & Fender Notes

Anchor center on hoods and work to corners; use knifeless tape at hood lines. Remove door handles when possible; watch door cup depth. On fenders, blend at the door gap with knifeless tape for a clean panel-line break.

---

## Session 7: Troubleshooting Common Defects

| Defect | Likely Cause | Fix |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| **Finger** | Insufficient stretch/heat on curve | Lift, reheat, re-squeegee; or remove and redo |
| **Silvering** | Overstretch or adhesive stress | Remove section; install fresh film |
| **Contamination** | Skipped decon step or dusty shop | Remove film, re-prep, reinstall |
| **Edge lift** | Incomplete post-heat or dirty edge | Re-squeegee, post-heat, edge seal if specified |
| **Stretch marks / color shift** | Film pulled too hard (especially metallic) | Remove and replace — marks don't heal |
| **Orange peel in adhesive** | Trapped application fluid | Squeegee harder; post-heat; severe cases = redo |

## Session 8: Wrap Packages, Inlays & Door Jambs

### Common Wrap Packages

| Package | Typical Coverage | Target Customer |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Accent / partial** | Roof, hood, mirrors, spoiler | Enthusiast entry point |
| **Two-tone / multi-panel** | Hood + fenders, or roof + pillars | Color-change without full vehicle cost |
| **Full color change** | Entire painted surface including jambs | Complete transformation |
| **Commercial / fleet** | Logos, lettering, partial branding | Business vehicles |
| **Chrome delete** | Window trim, grilles, badges | Popular upsell on dark vehicles |

### Door Jamb Coverage

Professional full color-change wraps include door jambs — the visible painted edges when doors are open.

**Door jamb workflow:** Open door → clean jamb thoroughly → apply smaller pieces with minimal stretch → wrap or knifeless-cut edges → post-heat all jamb sections.

<div class="callout tip">

**Upsell script:** "Wrapping the door jambs means when you open the door, you see the new color everywhere — not a factory color peeking through. It's the difference between a wrap job and a complete transformation."

</div>

### Inlay Technique

An **inlay** is a pre-trimmed piece set into a larger panel so seams fall on body lines — used at hood-to-fender lines, door cups, extreme bumper curves, and multi-color designs.

Plan seams on body lines or recesses — never across a flat visible panel. Overlap 3–5mm in hidden zones and post-heat all inlay edges.

---

## Session 9: Knifeless Tape Technique

Knifeless tape is the professional standard for safe, straight cuts at panel lines. Review the tool layout shown in the image from Session 3.

### Knifeless Tape Workflow

1. Clean panel line → apply tape (filament toward cut zone) → apply vinyl → post-heat → pull filament → remove excess → post-heat cut edge

<div class="callout warning">

**Do not pull the filament before post-heating** unless your film manufacturer specifies otherwise. Cutting cold film can leave ragged edges and incomplete adhesive contact.

</div>

Apply knifeless tape at hood-to-fender lines, door gaps, roof-to-pillar transitions, and factory bumper seam lines. Follow your shop's panel order workflow — fender-first or hood-first — consistently across the job.

## Session 10: QC Inspection & Customer Delivery

### QC Checklist — Every Install

<ul class="checklist">
<li>Paint prepped — decon and IPA complete; pre-existing damage documented</li>
<li>No contamination under film (inspect with light at angle)</li>
<li>No fingers, silvering, or stretch marks in visible areas</li>
<li>Edges wrapped, tucked, or knifeless-cut per spec — no lift</li>
<li>All panel pieces aligned — seams on body lines, not random locations</li>
<li>Door jambs complete (if in package) — no factory color showing</li>
<li>Emblems, badges, handles, and sensors properly handled</li>
<li>ADAS sensors uncovered and functional</li>
<li>Post-heat completed on all installed panels</li>
<li>Customer paperwork with package details, color/film info, and warranty info</li>
</ul>

### Customer Delivery

1. Walk the vehicle with the customer in good lighting
2. Point out coverage zones and documented pre-existing damage
3. Explain cure time: **no wash for 48–72 hours**; hand wash only for first week
4. Provide aftercare sheet and explain film vs. workmanship warranty

## Session 11: Pricing & Business Basics

### Pricing Components

| Component | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Material cost** | Film cost per sq ft × coverage area + 15–25% waste factor |
| **Labor** | Hours × shop rate (wrap labor rate is higher than tint) |
| **Complexity premium** | Exotics, deep bumpers, metallic/pearl films, jamb coverage |
| **Correction add-on** | Polish/decon if not included in base |
| **Removal** | Existing wrap or PPF removal billed separately |

### Sample Package Pricing Framework

| Package | Typical Retail Range* |
|---------|----------------------|
| Accent (roof or hood) | $400 – $900 |
| Multi-panel (hood + fenders) | $1,200 – $2,500 |
| Full color change (with jambs) | $3,500 – $7,000 |
| Full color change (exotic/large SUV) | $5,000 – $10,000+ |
| Chrome delete | $300 – $800 |

*\*Varies significantly by market, film type, and vehicle size. Research your local market.*

### Profitability Tips

- **Film efficiency** — nest cuts tightly; track waste per job; save usable offcuts for mirrors and accents
- **Bundle services** — wrap + PPF on high-impact zones; wrap + ceramic coating
- **Deposit policy** — 50% deposit on wrap bookings; material cost is high and non-returnable once cut
- **Photography** — before/after photos are your best marketing asset; shoot in consistent lighting

## Resources & Quick Reference

### Decon Sequence
Wash → Clay → Iron remover → Tar spot treat → IPA wipe → Inspect

### Stretch Rule
Anchor flat zone first → stretch only where needed → heat assist → post-heat all panels

### Edge Priority
Knifeless cut (safest at panel lines) → Tuck (best appearance) → Wrap (best durability) → Flush (hidden edges only)

### Post-Heat Target
~180–200°F · constant motion · all edges and stretched zones

### Aftercare
48–72 hrs no wash · Hand wash first week · No pressure on edges · Annual inspection recommended
